## Post 1

**Date:** 2026-06-10

**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/diandraescobar_we-were-sold-the-idea-that-posting-is-the-activity-7470457277410607105-HxIa

**Why selected:** Breaks down the full 9-step LinkedIn content chain from ICP to email list — the most complete B2B SaaS organic growth framework in the collection.

We were sold the idea that posting is the only thing standing between us and winning on LinkedIn. But posting is just ONE link in a chain.

And chains don't break at their strongest point.

They break at the weakest one.

Winning on LinkedIn has been sold to us as a big number. A viral post, a follower count, a screenshot of impressions.

Real winning is quieter. It's the right person reading you for weeks, never liking a thing, then showing up in your DMs ready to buy.

That only happens when they trust you.

And trust isn't one post, it's the same person seeing you be useful, over and over, until you're the obvious choice.

Every step below exists to build that trust and turn it into pipeline. Miss one and the chain breaks somewhere you won't notice until the leads don't come.

Here are the nine, and what each one actually gets you:

1. A defined ICP → a message that lands

Scream their pain point back at them, then drip solutions through your content. If you define who it's for, the words get sharper on their own.

2. A profile built for search and algorithm → profile traffic

Your profile is the conversion point. Every post is the ad that sends people there, so it has to sell when they arrive and it has to help the algorithm put you there.

3. Daily ICP comments → an audience

Posting to zero followers will burn you out, trust me. Comments are how you get in front of other people's audiences while building your own at the same time.

4. Consistency → compounding

One good post is luck. Consistency is what teaches people who you are. The more often the right people see you, the faster trust compounds.

5. Value or curiosity → a "see more"

The first lines have one job: earn the click. Give people a reason to stay curious, promise value, or open a gap they need closed.

6. A "how I" frame → trust

"How to" is advice anyone can give. "How I" is lived experience. The more specific your experience is, the harder it becomes to copy.

7. Saves and sends → a real signal

Likes are passive. Saves and sends tell the algorithm someone cared enough to come back to it or share it with someone else.

8. Conversion posts → DMs

At some point you have to ask for the business. Built on the first seven, asking feels natural instead of desperate.

9. A lead magnet → an email list

Attention you don't capture is attention you rent. A lead magnet turns a reader into someone you can reach again.

Most people want to start at step five, the viral hook.

The first four are boring, which is why almost nobody does them.

Get the foundations right, and everything after that gets easier.

## Post 2

**Date:** 2026-06-10

**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/diandraescobar_marketers-new-moral-stance-is-never-gating-activity-7468662926623715328-B9NK

**Why selected:** Real data on content gating vs. free content debate — 3,800 subscribers, 43% open rate, 101 paid community members in 2 months. Directly relevant to B2B SaaS content monetization strategy.

Marketers' new moral stance is never gating content. I gated every resource I have, and 2 months later my newsletter sits at 3,8k subscribers, 43% open rate and 8.9% click rate.

Some context: At distinctiva.io we write newsletters for clients every week, and I co-run theworkflow.digital with Sara.

But mine sat on the to-do list way too long, until I finally launched it on April 5th.

The girl finally ate her own dog food.

What's driving it:

1. Every resource sits behind an email gate. And I don't apologize for it.

I put out content I genuinely work hard on to help others: 5-7 times a week on LinkedIn, once a week on YouTube, every 2 weeks through Substack for The Workflow. All free. I won't apologize for gating one resource.

Because it's not a random PDF I whipped up in an afternoon. Every resource I gate is heavily researched, carefully built, and something I actually use myself. If it took me 20 hours to build, an email is a fair trade.

2. The newsletter is where I do my deepest thinking.

Most of my content flywheel starts in the newsletter or a YouTube script, because that's where I'm not fighting character limits. Step by step processes, screenshots, full workflows, nothing held back.

I don't send an issue unless it's been properly thought out. If I have nothing worth sending, I don't send.

3. Subject lines are hooks. I treat them exactly like LinkedIn hooks.

"I studied 131 hooks from creators who are actually growing" pulled a 44% open rate. Same psychology, different inbox.

4. When I sell, I just sell.

No burying the offer under five "value points." My promotional issues are direct: here's the community, here's the price, here's the deadline.

Result: 101 paid members in my community, and 80% of them came through email.

When you're useful, be the most useful. When you sell, go for the sale. Mixing the two weakens both.
