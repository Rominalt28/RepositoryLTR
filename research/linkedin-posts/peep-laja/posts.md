## Post 1

**Date:** 2026-06-17

**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/peeplaja_41-of-b2b-buyers-say-they-chose-a-vendor-activity-7472603887766544384-lpJ1

**Why selected:** Data from 100 marketing leaders shows only 41% chose a vendor for a genuinely different product — the other 59% decided on ease, trust, or price. The clearest argument for why B2B SaaS content must build brand familiarity before the buying decision starts.

41% of B2B buyers say they chose a vendor for a genuinely different product capability.

Which raises a harder question: what about the other 59%?

We asked 100 marketing leaders the single biggest reason they picked the winner.

Product capability led at 41%. But right behind it: easiest to buy from (24%), existing relationship or trust (21%), price (12%).

The easy story is that buyers are irrational. They say they want the best tool, then pick on feelings.

I don't buy that. These are senior operators.

Here's the likelier explanation: for most of them, a real product difference wasn't on the table. When you've already established the tools do basically the same thing, "which has the better capability" stops being a useful question.

So you fall back to what's left, who's easier to work with, who you trust, who costs less.

That's not buyers being soft. That's buyers being rational in a market where vendors failed to build, or failed to prove, a real difference.

The 59% is a differentiation problem. Or maybe brand opportunity.

## Post 2

**Date:** 2026-06-17

**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/peeplaja_64-of-buyers-said-it-is-hard-to-tell-competing-activity-7470791975546445824-yWxe

**Why selected:** 64% of buyers can't tell competing B2B vendors apart — and 44% gave up trying and bought on other factors. Direct proof that undifferentiated messaging kills content effectiveness before the demo ever happens.

64% of buyers said it is hard to tell competing vendors apart.

We used a Wynter survey to ask 100 B2B marketers about vendor comparison.

What the research shows is that most websites fail to communicate meaningful differentiation. Majority constantly thought "these all basically do the same thing".

When everything reads the same, buyers split. 56% dug deep to find a real difference. The other 44% gave up trying and decided on other factors.

One CMO stopped using vendor material entirely: "I stopped reading and using their info and looked to my peers for insights and recommendations on the tool(s) they use. That was far more informative than anything I got from any of the vendors."

So when the website doesn't make the difference clear, what closes the deal?

Two things, neck and neck.

1. 32% said it came down to fit.

Whoever proved they matched the exact use case, stack, and integrations won.

2. 31% said the sales experience was the real difference.

A rep who explained what the site couldn't. As one CMO put it: "their sellers were able to do a good job of explaining the differences that the website by itself was not able to do."

Companies are scared (or stupid?) to not focus on their differentiation. If they're lucky enough to still get a demo request, it's then up to the salespeople to explain it. But all but the best-known brands get just ignored.

## Post 3

**Date:** 2026-05-24

**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/peeplaja_b2b-marketing-budgets-keep-climbing-performance-activity-7457746061827293185-NP6p

**Why selected:** Q2 2026 Wynter Index across 200 B2B SaaS leaders: budgets are up across every lever but hitting plan is a coin flip. AI search is rerouting funnels, LinkedIn is polarizing, and paid is the fastest lever — not the best. A real-time read on where B2B SaaS marketing is breaking down.

B2B marketing budgets keep climbing. Performance doesn't.

Wynter Index Q2 2026 dropped today. 200 B2B SaaS marketing leaders surveyed.

Ad spend is the standout: roughly four times as many teams are raising paid budgets as cutting them. The fastest accelerator in the index, and still climbing.

Software spend, headcount, and agency spend all expanded too, but mildly. The buying spree on tools that defined Q1 has moderated.

Then there's performance to plan. Dead even. As many teams missed their number this quarter as hit it.

Spend is up across every lever. Hitting plan is a coin flip.

Three things explain it:

1. AI search is rerouting the funnel.

31% of marketers named AEO, GEO, or AI search erosion as a Q2 priority. A year ago this was a rounding error.

One Enterprise marketer at a former 90% SEO-driven shop: they've seen sharp drops in SEO traffic and revenue, and are pivoting their team toward holistic earned media plus AEO.

Another: "GEO pilots are delivering less traffic, but more highly engaged and ready to purchase users."

Top of funnel has moved to LLMs (and Gartner predicts LLMs will produce 95% in the future).

2. LinkedIn is the most polarizing channel in B2B.

38% of marketers are betting on LinkedIn this quarter. More than any other channel.

7% are explicitly cutting it. The most-named cut for ROI reasons.

Same channel. Top of the bet list AND the cut list.

A VP of Demand Gen: "CPL has increased significantly and the quality has been declining for some time. We are not seeing ROI."

LinkedIn is becoming a "you crack it or you bail" channel. There's no middle.

3. The paid lever is the shortest path to motion.

Marketers are under pressure to produce results, fast. Paid ads seems to be the fastest way to get there.

However marketers seem to be pulling the lever that moves fastest, not necessarily the one that moves furthest. Performance hasn't moved for most.
